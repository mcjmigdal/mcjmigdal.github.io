Brief overview - What is molecular docing?

Molecular docking is a technique/method used to predict structure of the intermolecular complex, formed between two or more molecules. [1] Those molecules could be ligand and some protein, but also complex of two proteins. The docking problem may be formally stated as falows: Let A,B be two molecules, than we would like to find a transformation T that the score measuring the quality of the proposed docking solution is maximized. [2] Looking at this definition we can find two key indigridents in molecular docking: transformation - so we need a way to generate or explore different structures of complex between two molecules, and a score that will tell us which of those structures is best. Before we look into these in more detail, I would like to tell more about what is molecular docking exacely by looking at some examples of its usage.

How it work's

As I already have told before. The docking procedure, or so called docking problem, can be divided into two:

  1. Prediction of the intermolecular complex structure
  2. Evaluation of the generated structure [1]

(1)The most intuitive approach to the first problem would be a hands-on approach (according to Leach) which requires a good idea of the expected binding mode, for example we already know the binding mode of a closely related ligand. However, it has been showed that even very similar inhibitors might adopt quite different binding modes. Moreover if one would want to dock large number of inhibitors by hand it would cost lot of time, not to mention that human modelers can be biased and programs usually can consider many more possibilities. [1]

The second problem comes from the fact that usually there are no simple answers and most docking algorithms generates a large number of possible structures, thus we need a way to score those different possibilities in order to select a single one. [1]

Docking alghoritms -- prediction of ligand pose

How complex the docking problem is?

Docking problem involves many degrees of freedom:

  • six degrees of translational and rotational freedom of one molecule relative to other
  • conformational degrees of freedom of each molecule

Such a large number of df, makes considering all of them probably impossible. Thus many different strategies have been devised to deal with that problem. Those strategies or alghoritms, can be characterized according to the number of df that they ignore. [1]

The simplest alghoritm treats the two molecules as rigid bodies and explores only the six degrees of translational and rotational freedom [1] More advanced method could treat one molecule as rigid and other as flexible and so on.

DOCK - one of earlies alghoritm example

Dock is one of the first docking alghoritms and a example of most simple one. This program was created in 1982 by Kuntz and co-workers, (note there were even earlier attempts) it was designed to find molecelues with a huge degree of shape complementarity to the binding site and this idea of using shape complementarity between two bodies is beeing inherited also by other newer programs.

::
To do so it first derives a 'negative image' of the binding site from the molecular surface of the macromolecule. This negative image consist of collection of overlapping spheres of varying radii, each of which touches the molecular surface at just two points. [1] Next stage is to look for clusters of those spheres, the existence of this kind of clusters indicates the existance of a cavity in the molecular surface, which is considered to be a good docking site. [2] Ligand atoms are then matched to the sphere centers to find sets of ligand atoms in which all the distances between the atoms in the set are equal to the corresponding sphere centre-sphere centre distances ( with some user predefined tollerance). [1] This is done only by translations and rotations. [2] The ligand can then be oriented withing the site by performing a least-squeres fit of the atoms to the sphere centers, then orientation is checked to ensure there are o unacceptable steric interactions between the ligand and the receptor. If the orientation is acceptable then an interaction energy is computed to give the 'score' for that binding mode. New orientations are generated by matching different sets of atoms and sphere centers. The top-scoring orienations are retained for subsequent analysis. [1] The main feature that should be noted here is that whole docking procedure is based on shape complementarity between two bodies.

Flexible docking alghoritms

As people tried to improve prediction capabilities of docking software they devised flexible docking. Performing conformationally flexible docking requires taking into account conformational df. Many of the methods tries to deal with this problem by considering only the conformational space of the ligand, while receptor is assumed to be rigid. [1] Hopefully to those programs this assumption of protein ridigity is often a fair aproximaton, as protein usually doesnt change much on binding ligand. Thus this comes to a similar problem as searching a optimal conformation.There are number of algorithms used to solve this kind of problem and their are used in molecular docking as well includong Monte Carlo, genetic algorithms, distance geometry,ant-colony optimization, etc., there is quite a number of these in fact and probably all of them have been used in some program. [1]

Although I sad that many programs consider only conformational space of ligand, this is not true for all of them. In fact number of programs that includes flexibility of protein and ligand is quite large already, however the degree to which proteins are consider flexible is varying from side-chains only in some part of protein to full protein flexibility. For example GalaxyDock accounts for protein flexibility of pre-selected residues, RosettaLigand implements full protein backbone and side-chain flexibility and even posibility to simultaneous dock multiple ligands. AutoDock takes into account flexibility by separating choosen side chains and making them part of ligand input. An so on numbers of different solutions are possible today (cIFD, RosettLigand, AutoDock, AutoDock Vina).[7]_

Methods combining docking with molecular dynamics are also available. Those are used when no
crystallographic data is available for a given target. In these approach accepted view is that
greater sampling of biomolecular space is desirable to find optimal solution. But the main
obstacle is short timescale accessible to MD simulations. These are tried to be overcome by
increasing speed and efficiency of both algorithms and computer hardware (ex. GPU accelerated
computing). One such example is MM2QM tool which allowed combining the docking, MD, MM and QM
calculations for either generation of alternative protein receptor conformations or
optimization of final docked structures. [7]_

For example, **Monte Carlo** methods have been used to perfom molecular docking, often in conjunction with
 simulated annealing. In this method at each iteration the interal conformation of the ligand is changed
 (by rotating about a bond) or the entire molecule is randomly translated or rotated. The energy of the
 ligand in the binding site is calculated using molecular mechanics and the move is then accepted or
 rejected.[1]_

Another example is the use of genetic algorithms, in this approach each chromosome codes not only for the
internal conformation of the ligand but also for the orientation of the ligand within the receptor site. Both
the orientation and the internal conformation will thus vary as the population evolve - the score of each
docked structure within the site acts as the fitness function used to select the individuals for the next
iteration. [1]_

Another approach used by many programs involves the incremental construction of the ligand. This is similar in
spirit to the depth-first systematic conformational search. The main difference is that in docking the
 conformational search is performed within the binding site. Typical incremental construction algorithm first
identifies one or more `base fragments` within the ligand. These base fragments are often chosen to be
reasonably significant, fairly rigid part of the molecule such as a ring system. The base frament(s) are
docked into the binding site and may then be clustered to remove similar orientations. Each docked orientation
of the base fragment then represents starting point for the conformational analysis of the rest of the ligand.
This approach might be quite time consuming - since it is necessary to perform the conformational analysis for
each orientation of the base fragments. However, it is often found that the protein provides a particulary
useful constraint, enabling the search tree to be pruned very effectively. [1]_

More theory - scoring Functions for Molecular Docking

Docking algorithms are generally capable to generate a large number of potential solutions. Some of these can be rejected immediately because they have a high-energy clash with the protein. But the remainder must be somehow assessed - this is done using scoring function. We should consider two situations when we scoring functions are used: [1]

  1. We are interested in how a single ligand binds to the protein
  2. We are interested in docking large number of molecules to protein of interest and deciding which one is the best match - virtual screening (VS).

a) In the first situation, scoring function must be able to identify the true docking mode of a given ligand, moreover this scoring function should be calculated rapidly because of large number of orientations that may be generated during docking run. b) However in second case, when docking more than one molecule -- say database of molecules, it also needs to be able to rank one ligand relative to another. [1]

Most commonly used are emiprical based scoring functions, they usually take into account the size of the contact surface between the two molecules and they do not allow one molecule to penetrate the other. They also tries to approximate the ligand-receptor binding energy using very fast and approximate method. Usually those consider free energy of binding written as an additive equation of various additive components to reflect various contribution to binding, example of how such equation could look like is presented at the slide.[2]_

Polish example - Fail Research by M. Bajda and others from Jagiellonian University was focused on acetlcholinesterase, enzyme responsible for acetlcholine hydrolisis in cholinergic synapses. According to one of hypothesis on Alzheimer's disease many symptoms of dementia are connected with low levels of acelcholine -- thus inhibitors of that enzyme are search as the potential drug against AD. Currently, three are 4 of those at the market (tacrine was first).

The aim of the study was to find more potent drug via virtual scanning techniques and ZINC database. The ZINC database is a free data set of commercially available compounds, frequently used to vritual screening. In contains over 35 million compounds. The VS done reasearchers was done in a multi-step manner. First they chosen only those compunds that had important physicochemical properties like proper absorption and distribution to central nervous system. In the next step they used information on structure of acetylcholinoesterase in complexes with four different inhibitors to create a pharmacophore models (Pharmacophore is an abstract description of molecular features that are necessary for molecular recognition of a ligand by a biological macromolecule) ,that purpose they used special computer program. Using that model they performed another screening on remaining compounds. Now the fit quality of the compounds to features described by a pharmacophore model was used as filtration criteria and allowed to choose 88 compounds for futher screening. This was done using molecular docking, and filtering criterion was score obtained with the ChemScore scoring function (which is popular scoring functions available in Discovery program). Number of compounds was further narrowed to 7, which was belived as resonable number. However this studies were unable to find any new potent drug, as all 7 compunds were less effective than drugs available on the marked, they nicely show the frame work. [5]

Successful example

Study by group from Italy was focused on finding new inhibitors mitochondrial ADP/ATP carriers that translocate the ATP synthetized within mitochondria to cytosol in exchangefor ADP. Since those proteins are dysregulated in several cancer types, they belive that employment of inhibitors specific to those carriers might be crucial for inducing apoptosis in cancer cells.

In order to find new potent inhibitors they constructed model of ADP/ATP carrier based on homological structure, from bull which shares ~90% of identical residues and using Modeller. As for collection of compounds to screen they have used KEGG ligand database, but only compounds greater than 10 atoms and lighter than 1200 g/mol. Docking-based virtual screening (Virtual screening is computional technique used in drug discovery to search libraries of small molecules in order to identify new potent drugs against some target) was conducted using AutoDock and took 2 months for completing the screening of the entire library. This allowed to choose 13 best molecules. In further research it was found that 2 of them showed good results and high selectivity, comparable or better that previously known inhibitors. [6]
